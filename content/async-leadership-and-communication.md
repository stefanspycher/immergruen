# Async Leadership and Communication

Effective distributed leadership defaults to async communication, not because it is more convenient, but because synchronicity is a coordination cost that must be justified.

## The Core Principle

> Distributed teams are better than co-located teams — not because they're distributed, but because they're asynchronous.

Asynchronous operation means people work in parallel, with as few synchronization points as necessary. This is not a compromise. It is a feature. It gives individuals control over their schedule, enables deeper work, and forces information to be written down where others can find it later.

Nothing has as dire an impact on productivity as poor communication. The inverse is not true: good communication does not guarantee success, but bad communication guarantees failure.

## Default to Written, Persistent Communication

A capable distributed team habitually externalizes information. The default communication medium should be:

1. **Wiki / shared documents**: for knowledge that needs to be found and referenced later
2. **Issue trackers**: for decisions and action items
3. **Async text channels**: only when immediate, interactive mutual feedback is required

### Chat is a shoulder tap

Sending someone a direct message or mention is the digital equivalent of walking up and interrupting them. It compels a response. Use it only when the topic genuinely requires real-time back-and-forth and the interruption cost is justified.

Never send a naked ping ("hey", "ping", "?"). Always include context and, if non-urgent, say so explicitly.

### Video calls: when and how

Video calls are useful for one thing other formats cannot replicate: picking up on nonverbal cues like body language, tone, and facial expression. That is the only reason to default to them.

Every video call requires:

- A circulated **agenda** in advance
- Written **notes and summary** afterward, circulated to all who need to know (not just attendees)
- The write-up takes _more_ effort than the call itself. That is the standard, not optional.

> Once you do meetings right, you no longer need most of them.

Recurring team meetings serve an emotional sync function, not an information function. One per week is usually sufficient; skipping when needed causes no harm if the team externalizes information well.

## Briefing People: The 5-Paragraph Format

For important briefings to a group, use this structure:

1. **Situation**: where we are and why we are doing this
2. **Objective**: what we want to achieve
3. **Plan**: how we will achieve it
4. **Logistics**: budget, resources, constraints
5. **Communications**: how we coordinate and report

Always schedule a follow-up round after briefings: people need time to form questions. Use that session to verify comprehension by asking specific questions about what was covered.

## Escalation as a Leadership Tool

Escalation is widely perceived as a sign of failure or weakness. This framing is incorrect and harmful.

Escalation has two distinct meanings:

1. The _worsening_ of a conflict (the common negative connotation)
2. The _referral_ of an issue to a higher decision-making level (a neutral coordination tool)

The negative connotation causes leaders to avoid escalating when they should, leaving priority conflicts unresolved and problems invisible to those who could fix them. Escalation is a tool that resolves conflicts that cannot be handled at the current level, creates visibility on challenges, and signals good judgment about where decisions belong.
