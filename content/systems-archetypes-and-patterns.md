# Systems Archetypes and Patterns

Systems archetypes are recurring structures in complex systems: the same patterns of relationships producing the same characteristic behaviors, across different organizations, industries, and contexts. Recognizing which archetype is at play turns a confusing situation into a tractable one.

## Why Archetypes Matter

Most organizational problems are not unique. They are instances of a small set of recurring structures. The value of naming the archetype is not elegance; it is that each archetype has known intervention points, and the wrong interventions are also known.

Failure to recognize archetypes leads to applying the wrong fix, reinforcing the problem, or treating symptoms rather than structure.

## Key Archetypes

### Limits to Growth

A reinforcing (accelerating) process is slowed, then stopped, then reversed by a balancing constraint. The natural response, pushing harder, accelerates the slowdown.

_Intervention:_ Identify and address the limiting constraint. Pushing harder on growth without removing the constraint only makes things worse.

### Shifting the Burden

A symptom is addressed with a quick fix rather than a fundamental solution. The quick fix reduces pressure to address the root cause, which then weakens over time. Dependency on the symptomatic solution grows.

_Example:_ Addressing team overload by adding people instead of reducing scope. The added people create coordination overhead, which increases complexity, which creates more overload.

_Intervention:_ Make the symptomatic fix less accessible, or make the fundamental fix more visible and achievable.

### Tragedy of the Commons

Multiple actors share a common resource. Each actor benefits individually from using more of it, but the total cost is distributed across all actors. Rational individual behavior produces collective destruction.

_Application:_ Identify your organization's shared resources: time, attention, trust, infrastructure capacity. Ask what behaviors are depleting them, and what incentives drive those behaviors.

### Fixes That Fail

A fix addresses a symptom effectively in the short term but produces unintended consequences that eventually recreate or worsen the original problem.

_Intervention:_ Extend your time horizon. Ask what the second-order effects of the proposed fix will be.

### Eroding Goals

When the gap between desired state and current state becomes uncomfortable, the response is to lower the goal rather than improve performance. Standards drift downward over time.

_Intervention:_ Hold goals fixed; make the gap visible and uncomfortable; improve conditions rather than adjusting expectations.

## Ackoff's F-Laws: Epigrams on Organizational Reality

Ackoff distilled decades of observation into short epigrams. The most durable:

**On optimization and parts:**

- You rarely improve an organisation as a whole by improving the performance of one or more of its parts.
- It is better to dissolve a problem than to solve it.
- It is better to control the future imperfectly than to forecast it perfectly.
- The future is better dealt with using assumptions than forecasts.

**On knowledge and learning:**

- In a classroom, the teacher learns most.
- Good teachers produce sceptics who ask their own questions; gurus produce only unquestioning disciples.
- Curiosity is the open sesame to learning.
- Benchmarking is a subtle form of imitation. It condemns organisations to following, not leading.
- The best way to find out how to get from here to there is to find out how to get from there to here.

**On management behavior:**

- When managers say something is obvious, it does not mean it is unquestionable. It means they are unwilling to have it questioned.
- The less managers understand their business, the more variables they require to explain it.
- Managers who do not know how to measure what they want settle for wanting what they can measure.
- Those who successfully managed a company to maturity are unlikely to be able to manage it back to youth.

**On structure and change:**

- The only thing more difficult than starting something new in an organisation is stopping something old.
- In an organisation that disapproves of mistakes but identifies only errors of commission, the optimal strategy is to do nothing.
- Complex problems do not have simple solutions.
- Viewing things differently is not a defect: it is an advantage.

## Applying Archetypes

When facing a persistent organizational problem:

1. Ask: have we seen this pattern before, in a different context?
2. Map the reinforcing and balancing loops at play.
3. Identify which archetype fits the structure.
4. Find the intervention point the archetype implies, and resist the intervention it warns against.
