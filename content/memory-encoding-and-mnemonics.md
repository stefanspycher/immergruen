# Memory Encoding and Mnemonics

Memory encoding is the process of converting information into a form the brain retains reliably. Raw information, especially abstract or numerical content, is difficult to encode directly. Mnemonic systems convert it into forms the brain handles well: imagery, narrative, spatial relationships.

## Why Encoding Works

The brain retains information better when it is:

- **Concrete** rather than abstract
- **Connected** to existing memories rather than isolated
- **Distinctive**: unusual or vivid images are harder to forget than neutral ones
- **Spatial**: information placed in mental locations can be retrieved by traversing those locations

Mnemonic systems exploit these properties deliberately.

## The P.A.O. System (Person-Action-Object)

The PAO system encodes numbers by converting digit pairs into a memorable three-element sentence: a **Person**, performing an **Action**, on or with an **Object**.

Structure:

- Every digit pair (00-99) is assigned a Person, an Action, and an Object
- A 6-digit number becomes three 2-digit chunks: Person (first two) + Action (middle two) + Object (last two)
- The result is a single vivid image: e.g., 234567 becomes "Einstein (23) juggling (45) a piano (67)"

The system requires building a lookup table of 100 persons, 100 actions, and 100 objects with consistent associations. Once built, it enables rapid encoding of arbitrary number sequences.

PAO images can be chained, mixed (one person's action with another's object), or placed in memory palace locations for larger datasets. Most effective for phone numbers, dates, codes, and any domain requiring reliable retention of digit sequences.

## Wozniak's Encoding Principles

From the 20 rules of knowledge formulation, these focus specifically on encoding into long-term memory:

**Use imagery:** A picture is worth a thousand words. Concrete visual representations are retained far better than abstract descriptions. Graphic deletion (obscuring parts of an image) works as well as cloze deletion for visual information.

**Use cloze deletion:** Completing a deleted word or phrase is more effective than re-reading. "The capital of France is [...]" is more retainable than "The capital of France is Paris." Cloze deletion greatly speeds up knowledge formulation, especially for beginners.

**Avoid sets; convert to enumerations:** Sets (unordered collections) are nearly impossible to memorize reliably. Converting to ordered enumerations makes them tractable. Even enumerations are difficult; use cloze deletion or narrative to encode them.

**Combat interference:** Similar items interfere with each other in memory. Countermeasures include using different examples, different contexts, vivid and distinctive illustrations, and personal associations. Interference is the most common reason simple items fail to stick despite repeated exposure.

**Rely on emotional states:** Emotions are linked to memory: learning in a particular emotional state improves recall in that same state. Strong emotional associations make memories more durable.

## Combining Techniques

PAO + memory palace: place PAO images in spatial locations (rooms, landmarks, familiar routes) for very large datasets. Retrieval involves mentally traversing the space and reading the images encountered.

Cloze deletion + personalization: format knowledge as fill-in-the-blank, but use personal examples as the context. The combination of active recall and personal relevance produces durable retention.

## The Investment Tradeoff

Mnemonic systems require upfront investment: building the PAO table, creating imagery, establishing spatial locations. This investment pays off for information that genuinely needs to be recalled without lookup.

The deciding question: is this information I need in my head, or can it be looked up? Encode only what needs to be immediately accessible.
