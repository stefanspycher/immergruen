# Information Quality and Access

The common diagnosis of information problems in organizations is wrong. The problem is not too much information, and it is not too little. The problem is **the wrong information reaching the wrong people at the wrong time.**

## What Actually Goes Wrong

Over-communication is as harmful as under-communication, possibly more so, because it is harder to recognize. When everything is communicated, important signals are buried in the stream of unimportant ones. Recipients develop coping mechanisms: they skim, filter, and ignore. Those mechanisms are imprecise. Critical information is lost alongside the noise.

The effect: as volume increases, the effective throughput of important information decreases.

## The Solution: Self-Service, Not Broadcast

Information should be **pull, not push**. The goal is a system where information is collected and organized in a central, accessible location, quality standards govern what is added and how it is structured, information is tagged and searchable at any time, recipients can react to and comment on information in context, and no one needs to remember or guess where a piece of information lives.

This is the opposite of broadcast communication. It assumes that recipients will seek information when they need it, provided it is findable when they look.

## Quality Standards for Information

Not all information deserves equal treatment. Quality standards should govern:

- **Relevance**: does this need to be in the shared system, or is it personal and local?
- **Accuracy**: is this verified, or is it someone's current understanding?
- **Timeliness**: is this current? Is it dated so recipients know when it was written?
- **Findability**: is this tagged and structured so it can be located without prior knowledge of where it lives?

Without quality standards, a central knowledge base degrades into structured noise rather than structured signal.

## Analytics as a Feedback Loop

Knowing what information is accessed, when, and by whom closes the loop between producers and consumers. Analytics on information access enables identifying what is actually used versus what is created and ignored, surfacing gaps (topics people search for but do not find), and pruning content that has become stale or irrelevant.

Without this feedback, information systems grow without any mechanism for contraction. They accumulate content until they become as hard to use as the broadcast channels they were meant to replace.

## The Leadership Question

Information architecture is a leadership decision, not a tooling decision. The tools matter less than the answers to:

- Who is responsible for the quality of shared information?
- What is the standard for something being ready to share?
- How does outdated information get removed or updated?
- How do people find what they need without asking a person?
