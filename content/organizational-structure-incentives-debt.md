# Organizational Structure, Incentives, and Debt

Organizations have two largely invisible forces that shape outcomes more than strategy or talent: the structure of their communication, and the structure of their incentives. Both tend to resist change, and both accumulate debt when left unexamined.

## Conway's Law and Organizational Debt

Conway's Law states that organizations design systems that mirror their communication structure. The inverse creates a specific failure mode: when a system's architecture fundamentally changes while the organizational structure remains static, severe misalignment results.

This misalignment is **organizational debt**: structure optimized for old architecture becomes an impediment to the new direction.

**Symptoms of organizational debt:**

- Teams organized around deprecated components or functions
- Unclear ownership of new architectural or strategic elements
- High coordination overhead for changes that should be simple
- Resistance to change because it threatens team identity and purpose
- People whose careers are built around expertise in what is being replaced

When architecture changes significantly, organizational restructuring is not optional; it is a consequence. Delaying it allows the debt to compound. Treat organizational structure as malleable, not permanent. Anticipate resistance (change is threatening to those invested in the current structure) and provide migration paths rather than simply eliminating roles.

## Incentive Misalignment Undermines Transformation

Organizations frequently reward individual achievement while expecting collaborative behavior. This creates a fundamental conflict that no amount of cultural rhetoric can resolve: **incentives win.**

The pattern:

- Transformation initiatives require fluid teams, knowledge sharing, and collaborative behavior
- Performance reviews, promotions, and recognition systems reward individual contributions and personal visibility
- Rational actors optimize for what is measured and rewarded
- Result: people compete where the organization needs them to cooperate

Observable behaviors driven by misalignment:

- Optimizing for individual visibility over team outcomes
- Resisting collaborative models that dilute personal credit
- Hoarding knowledge as competitive advantage
- Performing collaboration without practicing it

The solution is structural, not cultural:

1. Explicitly measure and reward collaborative behaviors in performance reviews
2. Make team outcomes visible and recognized alongside individual contributions
3. Use peer recognition and 360 reviews to surface collaborative behavior
4. Have leadership model the behaviors they are asking for

> You get what you measure and reward. If only individual success counts, collaboration remains aspiration, not reality.

## The Fragility of High-Performing Teams

High-performing teams operate through a reinforcing system of shared decision-making, mutual commitment, and external support from leadership. This system is effective but structurally fragile.

Key vulnerabilities:

- **Consensus has a velocity cost.** Getting every voice heard, considered, and integrated takes time. This is the price of genuine buy-in.
- **Mutual commitment is expensive to reach, and therefore expensive to revise.** When circumstances change, commitments built at high cost will be defended past their usefulness.
- **External support quality matters.** Team performance depends partly on the consistency of support from outside the team: attention, resources, clarity. Inconsistent inputs destabilize internal dynamics.
- **Scale changes everything.** A team model that works for a small, tightly-knit group does not automatically scale. The same dynamics that create cohesion at small scale can create insularity and slow decision-making at large scale.

These are not arguments against high-performing team models. They are the structural risks that require active management.
