# Libin's Communication Pyramid

Phil Libin, co-founder of Evernote and later CEO of video platform Mmhmm, ran a fully distributed company of ~150 people with no headquarters. He structured all communication as a pyramid with three tiers, each serving a distinct purpose.

## The pyramid

**Top (approx. 10%) — Physical meetings**
Reserved for starting creative processes: kicking off a project, generating ideas together. When you meet in person, do it somewhere worth being. Libin's company used an art museum for a product design offsite. Boring meeting rooms with fluorescent lighting are the worst possible use of physical presence.

**Middle (approx. 30%) — Synchronous video calls**
For small-group interaction, Q&A, and quick clarification. Good for things that need real back-and-forth but don't require being in the same room.

**Bottom (majority) — Async recorded video**
The default medium for most work. Used to convey complex information, develop ideas, and share concepts across time zones. Recorded video beats email for nuanced content: emotions are hard to read in text and easily misunderstood, while facial expressions carry them naturally. The viewer can pause, rewind, or speed up.

> "It takes longer to explain something in an email than in a video. Most people are natural video creators. Writing well is rare."
> — Phil Libin, NZZ interview, September 2022

## Why this matters

The pyramid makes explicit what most teams leave implicit: not all communication modes are interchangeable. Each format has different strengths. Defaulting to sync calls for work that could be a recorded video wastes time and adds unnecessary scheduling overhead. Defaulting to email for complex topics causes misunderstandings.

The physical tier is not eliminated, it is reserved for the one thing it does best: sparking creativity and building relationships. After that initial spark, async video scales the work to a global team without requiring everyone to be available at the same time.
